Every GPU-Hour Has an Opportunity Cost Now: How Compute Allocation Became the AI Valuation Game

For the last 25 years, internet companies grew on one economic fact: the marginal cost of serving one more user was close to zero. One more Gmail account, one more Facebook scroll, one more YouTube view — each cost Google and Meta almost nothing to serve. When serving the next user is free, the strategy writes itself: grab as many users as possible, monetize them later. This is how Uber grew. How Netflix grew. How every free-to-use consumer app since 2005 grew.

AI broke this assumption. Every ChatGPT message consumes a measurable fraction of a GPU-hour, and that GPU-hour has a real price — between $1.50 and $7 depending on who you rent from. The marginal cost of one more user is no longer zero. It is non-trivial, it compounds, and it has a name on the balance sheet.

When marginal cost stops being zero, a second cost starts to matter: opportunity cost. If serving this user costs a real GPU-hour, then every hour spent on one thing is an hour not spent on another. The strategic question shifts from "how do we acquire more users?" to "of everything this GPU-hour could be doing, what returns the most?"

This shift is already visible in the earnings calls. Microsoft has $400 billion of signed contracts it cannot deliver fast enough, and its CEO publicly admits the company turns away paying customers because the same compute is worth more inside Copilot. Alphabet raised its 2025 capex from $53 billion to $93 billion and still cannot build capacity fast enough to match demand. These are not growth numbers. They are allocation numbers.

Six Archetypes of Opportunity-Cost Strategy

Under opportunity-cost economics, every AI company falls into one of six archetypes based on what it owns in the stack: silicon, cloud, first-party product, distribution. The archetype determines which strategic options are even available. Model quality does not change it. Harness quality does not change it. Stack position does.

Here are the six.

The Upseller — Microsoft

Microsoft figured out something that, in hindsight, seems obvious: why sell a GPU-hour for $3–7 to an Azure customer when you can wrap that same hour into a $30-per-month Copilot subscription and get dramatically more out of it?

Fifteen million enterprise seats are now paying $30 a month for M365 Copilot, generating roughly $5.4 billion in annualized revenue — up 160% in a single year. A GPU-hour serving dozens of concurrent Copilot users contributes to a revenue stream directionally an order of magnitude larger than the same hour rented as raw Azure compute. Microsoft does not publish per-GPU Copilot economics, but the revealed preference is loud: CFO Amy Hood confirmed that Azure capacity is being prioritized for Copilot, security, and GitHub over third-party customers. The CEO is on the record saying the company turns away paying Azure demand because serving it "is not in our long-term interest."

Read that sentence again. Microsoft is choosing to lose Azure revenue today because the same silicon generates more value inside Copilot.

The risk is straightforward: the Azure customers getting rationed today have alternatives. If enough of them defect to GCP or AWS before Copilot retention matures, Microsoft will have traded a large base of loyal IaaS customers for a SaaS bet that has not yet proven sticky at scale.

The Flywheel — Alphabet

Alphabet is the only player in this game that owns the entire vertical. They design their own silicon (TPUs). They run their own cloud (GCP). They operate their own consumer AI products (Gemini, AI Overviews). And they sit on top of the largest advertising business in history — $300 billion and growing.

This means something remarkable: GPU-hours routed to any part of Alphabet's stack feed the same ad flywheel. Gemini processes 7 billion tokens per minute. The Gemini App has 650 million monthly active users. Google is running inference at ChatGPT-scale without paying anyone else's compute-rental tax, because they own the chips underneath.

But there is a counterintuitive wrinkle. AI Overviews — the AI-generated summaries that now sit above traditional Search results — may depress the per-query click yield of classic blue-link ads. Google is spending compute to potentially cannibalize its own highest-margin product.

Why would they do this? Because letting a competitor cannibalize it first is worse. Alphabet is racing against itself, because racing against anyone else would be losing.

The Kingmaker — Amazon

Amazon is making the strangest bet of any hyperscaler.

Through Project Rainier, Amazon dedicated approximately 500,000 Trainium2 chips to a single customer: Anthropic. Not to thousands of AWS enterprise customers. Not to the Bedrock inference platform. To one company. With plans to scale that allocation to one million chips.

In Q3 2025 alone, Amazon booked a $9.5 billion pretax gain on its Anthropic stake. That is more than those 500,000 chips would have earned running inference for third-party customers over the same period. The equity appreciation from a single quarter beat the IaaS yield.

But the equity gain is only half the logic. Every chip Anthropic uses for frontier-model training is also a reference deployment for Trainium, Amazon's custom silicon. If Trainium can train Claude, it can train anything — and that is the pitch Amazon needs to sell Trainium to the rest of the market. Amazon is paying opportunity cost in compute-hours and redeeming it in both equity and silicon credibility.

The risk is concentration. The whole strategy rests on one counterparty. If Anthropic falters, the equity unwinds and the Trainium reference story breaks in the same quarter.

The Fortress — Meta

Meta has no cloud business. There is no "Meta Cloud" you can buy GPU-hours from. Every chip Meta buys goes directly into Instagram ranking, Reels recommendations, Advantage+ ad optimization, or Meta Superintelligence Labs training runs.

This is the structurally simplest position in the AI economy. In Q3 2025, AI ranking improvements alone drove time-on-Facebook up 5% and time-on-Threads up 10%. Revenue hit $51.2 billion at a 40% operating margin across 3.54 billion daily active users. Meta does not need to ask the question that keeps every other hyperscaler's CFO awake: "should we sell this compute externally, or use it internally?" There is no external. There is only the product.

When the operator and the customer are the same entity, opportunity cost collapses to a single, much simpler question: of all the things we could be ranking better right now, which one moves revenue the most?

Meta raised $30 billion in bonds to fund 2025 capex. It has $40 billion in trailing free cash flow. No allocation guilt. No capacity rationing announcements. No Nadella-style admissions about turning customers away.

The twist: Meta is now also buying supplementary capacity from third-party clouds. It has become a compute-buyer with no compute-seller position to offset. A different kind of dependency, but dependency nonetheless.

The Preferred Tenant — Anthropic

Anthropic does not own silicon. It does not run a cloud. By the framework of this article, it should be in the same position as OpenAI: a renter at the mercy of its landlords.

It is not.

Anthropic made itself the tenant every landlord wants. AWS dedicated 500,000 Trainium2 chips through Project Rainier because Anthropic validates Trainium as frontier-grade silicon. Google committed up to one million TPUs because Anthropic validates TPU productization for external customers. Microsoft signed a $30 billion Azure commitment. CoreWeave signed a multi-year GPU cloud deal. Four providers, each with their own strategic reason to prioritize Anthropic's workloads over other customers.

The numbers reflect this leverage. Run-rate revenue crossed $30 billion in early 2026, roughly triple what it was at the end of 2025. When AWS experienced an outage recently, Claude kept running because traffic shifted to Google infrastructure without interruption.

Anthropic is also starting to build beyond renting. A $50 billion data-center partnership with Fluidstack will bring custom facilities online throughout 2026, and the company is in early-stage exploration of designing its own chips. Neither move is close to production, but both signal that Anthropic understands the preferred-tenant position is powerful but not permanent.

The risk: training frontier models across Trainium, TPUs, NVIDIA GPUs, and CoreWeave simultaneously requires engineering investment that single-cloud labs avoid. And four landlords competing for your tenancy today could become less generous when their own first-party models catch up.

The Exposed — OpenAI

OpenAI has 800 million weekly active users. Roughly 5% of them pay.

Sit with that for a moment. Approximately 760 million people use ChatGPT every week on compute that OpenAI rents, and those users generate zero revenue. Every free message competes internally with paid API tokens for the same rented GPU pool. Estimated annual compute costs run around $17 billion against roughly $13 billion in annualized revenue.

The company spends more on rented compute than it earns from customers.

When the other archetypes face compute scarcity, they reallocate. Microsoft moves GPU-hours from Azure customers to Copilot. Amazon moves GPU-hours from customers to Anthropic. These are trades between options, and they can be reversed. OpenAI's only immediate tool is cruder: on April 26, 2026, it shut down Sora to redirect rented compute toward coding agents and enterprise APIs. Token usage across the API had jumped from 6 billion per minute in October to 15 billion by March. CFO Sarah Friar told the Wall Street Journal she spends most of her time hunting for near-term compute capacity.

OpenAI has announced massive infrastructure plans: the $500 billion Stargate project with SoftBank and Oracle, and a custom inference chip codenamed Titan with Broadcom. But the first Stargate sites are not yet online, and Titan is not in production until late 2026. The Sora shutdown happened because OpenAI is, right now, a pure renter. The announced plans are the company's admission that the renter position is unsustainable at frontier scale.

Which Positions Survive 2028?

Six archetypes today. Not six in 2028. Opportunity-cost logic is dynamic. The GPU-hour that is worth more inside Copilot than on Azure today may be worth more in something else by 2027. Here is how each position holds up as the environment moves.

Alphabet (Flywheel): most durable. TPU production is scaling, Gemini is reaching ChatGPT volumes without paying someone else's rent, and the ad business generates enough cash to absorb any AI Overviews cannibalization. The only scenario that breaks this position is regulatory fragmentation of search, and even then the underlying stack stays intact.

Meta (Fortress): durable near-term, watch 2027. No external compute customers means no allocation conflicts today. But training frontier models is getting more expensive faster than ad revenue is growing, and Meta has already started buying external capacity. If that trend accelerates, the closed-loop advantage narrows into a closed-loop dependency.

Microsoft (Upseller): binary. The bet is that Copilot retention reaches a level where the Azure revenue sacrificed to rationing is permanently replaced by higher-margin SaaS. If Copilot churn proves worse than expected at the 18-month enterprise renewal cycle, this trade reverses publicly and messily. Watch the FY2027 Q2 earnings call.

Amazon (Kingmaker): concentrated. The position works if Anthropic stays frontier-grade. If Claude falls behind Gemini or GPT on any meaningful dimension for two quarters, the equity gain unwinds and Trainium loses its flagship reference customer in the same window. The AWS base business is a buffer, but the strategic bet is genuinely concentrated on a single counterparty.

Anthropic (Preferred Tenant): decays through 2028. Four landlords compete for Anthropic's workloads today because none of them has a competitive first-party model. That is the temporary condition. Gemini is already closing the gap. AWS will eventually need to defend Bedrock's in-house models. The Fluidstack partnership and the chip exploration are Anthropic's own acknowledgment that the leverage has a shelf life. The open question is not whether it erodes, but how much runway it buys to build real infrastructure ownership before it does.

OpenAI (Exposed): fragile until Stargate lands. The first Stargate sites come online in 2026 to 2027. Titan ships in late 2026. Neither helps in the next four quarters. If the compute crunch tightens meaningfully in 2026, OpenAI hits the wall before its own infrastructure arrives. The Sora shutdown is the preview, not the exception.

One pattern cuts across all six. The most durable positions are not the ones with the best models. They are the ones where the highest-value internal use of a GPU-hour exceeds the market price for that same hour, and where the company owns enough of the stack that the gap is unlikely to close.

What This Means for Engineers

If you build on any of these platforms, opportunity-cost logic is now your problem too. When Microsoft rations Azure capacity, Copilot gets compute first. When AWS rations, Anthropic's Rainier cluster takes priority. You are not a customer in the traditional sense anymore. You are a bidder in an internal auction you never knew you entered, competing against your vendor's own products for the same silicon.

Three concrete moves that take this seriously.

Build the model-agnostic router before you need it. The goal is that when one provider hits a capacity event (not a full outage, just degraded latency or throttled throughput), traffic shifts without a redeploy. The minimum viable version: an abstraction layer that accepts a request, selects a model based on current health signals (p95 latency, error rate, token throughput), and falls back on degradation. Anthropic's own four-provider training posture is the production proof. When AWS had its October outage, Claude kept serving because traffic moved to Google. That architecture is not exotic. It is a router, health checks, a fallback table, a feature flag. Teams that wait until a capacity crunch forces the migration pay for it at the worst possible moment.

Model your reserved-vs-on-demand breakeven, then commit. Reserved instances and committed-use contracts exist precisely for the scenario this article describes. The break-even logic is unambiguous: if expected utilization is above roughly 60 to 70 percent for one-year commits, or higher for three-year, committed capacity pays off before the term ends. The less obvious benefit is queue priority. In a capacity-constrained environment, committed customers get served first, and on-demand customers absorb the rationing. If your architecture cannot tolerate capacity rationing, you are buying on-demand because it is easier to reason about, not because it is cheaper. Run the math. Sign the contract.

Instrument cost-per-feature-shipped, including compute. Most engineering teams track ship velocity. Few track the compute spend that underwrote each shipped feature. Teams that combine both numbers make dramatically better prioritization calls. Concretely: tag every GPU-hour in your experimentation and production environments with the feature or experiment it supports. Roll up monthly by feature. Ratio that against revenue impact or user activation. The insights are often unpleasant. The fine-tuning run that cost $40K of compute for a 0.3 percent accuracy gain on a feature with 200 monthly active users was probably not worth it. You only know once you measure. Nadella-style scarcity reasoning should operate inside your sprint planning, not just inside hyperscaler earnings calls.

One pattern worth watching: the Sora shutdown. If your cloud vendor ships a consumer product running on the same infrastructure that serves your enterprise workload, assume your priority degrades the moment that product scales. OpenAI's Sora went from viral launch to shutdown in under a year because the compute it consumed was worth more elsewhere. Bedrock customers had latency quietly deprioritized during Project Rainier's scale-up. Azure customers had capacity explicitly rationed when Copilot needed it. Your workload is not immune. Negotiate capacity guarantees, sign reserved-instance contracts, or build migration paths before a consumer product launches on shared infrastructure. Once the priority inversion happens, unwinding it is expensive.

Compute allocation is now strategy at every level of the stack, all the way down to the sprint backlog. The companies that thrive are not the ones with the cheapest per-token cost or the biggest GPU pool. They are the ones who know, for every hour, what alternative use they are choosing against, and whether that choice still makes sense.
